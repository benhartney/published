A composite statistic (index) is formed when individual indicators are compiled into a single number.

The most well known example is the UN's Human Development Index (HDI), which measures aggregates the achievements in a country across three basic dimensions of human development: a long and healthy life, access to knowledge and a decent standard of living.

We can use these indexes to determine the state of a country (against the maximum possible score), compare countries, and identify trends over time for a country.

These indexes are essentially a political view. Any time we refer to the HDI's rankings, we are accepting the variables it is counting, the relative weightings between them, and various other implementation details.

They have issues (over-simplification, data quality etc), but to the extent that we already use them, we should have a clearer sense of these details.

But furthermore, we should be able to change them. We should be able to add other variables. We should be able to save these new indexes we create, and use them as proxies for our political views.

This is an experiment in doing those things.

Currently, this system allows you to edit the HDI. In the future, you will be able to create your own index, and share it. Want to add in things like measures of freedom, or carbon emissions, or inequality? You'll be able to do so.

Imagine someone that earns $30,000 each year.

If you think each of those dollars should contribute to their country's score equally, uncheck the box.

If you think that an extra dollar of income is worth much more to a poor person than a rich one - that is, the ability to transform additional income into equivalently valuable functionings declines as incomes rise - and thus you think that each of those dollars of income should be worth less than the one before it, check the checkbox.

The second $10,000 would be worth 15% of the first $10,000. The third $10,000 would be worth 9% of the first $10,000. And so on.
These are some examples of the types of variables that could be added to the scoring calculation, in a future update. Their presence here is not necessarily an endorsement of their importance - in theory, all possible components of a political view should be available to add to the calculation.
